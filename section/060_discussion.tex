\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Fundamental limits of hierarchical memory orchestration}

The regime-dependent framework establishes a practically important negative
result: beyond regime boundaries, deeper orchestration can predictably
\emph{increase} latency and energy expenditure by exposing synchronization and
transfer overheads without improving throughput.
In capacity-limited regimes, the absence of effective locality imposes
unavoidable memory-access costs that coordination cannot offset.
In I/O-limited regimes, physical bandwidth constraints dominate execution once
cross-tier transfer approaches sustained limits; any additional coordination
overhead is additive rather than compensatory.
This is qualitatively analogous to phase-transition phenomena in physical
systems, where control parameters govern discontinuous changes in dominant
behavior---an analogy that motivated our dimensionless-ratio formulation and
may extend the regime framework to other computational settings beyond AI
inference.

\subsection{Reconciling conflicting results in prior systems research}

A regime-based lens provides a unifying explanation for heterogeneous---and
sometimes contradictory---results in prior systems work.
Offloading, caching, and swapping techniques that implicitly assume operation
within coordination-dominated regimes deliver substantial gains when that
assumption holds.
When deployed in configurations where capacity or bandwidth constraints
dominate, their effectiveness diminishes sharply.
Differences in fast-memory capacity, sustained transfer bandwidth, and
workload characteristics can place nominally similar systems in different
regimes, producing divergent benchmark outcomes without obvious explanation.
Making the operating regime explicit therefore directly improves the
interpretability and generalisability of inference systems research.

\subsection{Persistence of regime structure under hardware advances}

A natural counterargument is that emerging memory technologies---HBM capacity
expansion, CXL-based memory pooling, disaggregated memory---will render regime
boundaries irrelevant.
Our analysis shows that these technologies can \emph{shift} boundaries by
increasing intermediate capacity or improving sustained bandwidth, but they do
not remove the underlying phase structure.
Once transfer demand approaches a tier's sustained bandwidth ceiling, or once
working-set residency fails in fast memory, the dominant term in the latency
decomposition changes discontinuously regardless of the absolute capacity or
bandwidth values.
Similarly, more sophisticated software strategies---aggressive prefetching,
deeper computation--transfer overlap---can redistribute visible latency within
coordination-dominated regimes but cannot violate physical capacity and
sustained-bandwidth limits.
Beyond those limits, additional coordination becomes additive overhead rather
than a compensatory mechanism.

\subsection{Regime-aware inference: a practical path forward}

The regime-dependent framework directly motivates a new class of adaptive
inference systems.
Rather than applying a fixed orchestration strategy uniformly, a regime-aware
controller can sample the latency decomposition
($T_{\mathrm{mem}},\,T_{\mathrm{swap}},\,T_{\mathrm{sync}}$) at low overhead
and classify the current operating regime---a task our prototype decision-tree
classifier accomplishes with $>$93\% accuracy across diverse workloads and
negligible runtime cost.
When the detector identifies an I/O-limited transition, reduced swap depth
recovered 14.2\% of latency in our preliminary experiments, suggesting that
reactive regime adaptation is both feasible and effective.
Developing production-grade online regime detectors and adaptive orchestration
policies represents the most direct path toward inference systems that reliably
avoid predictable failure zones.

\subsection{Limitations and future directions}

This study has limitations that motivate future research.
First, while validation spans GPU, TPU, Inferentia, AMD, and Optane platforms,
large-scale multi-node clusters introduce additional coordination layers
(inter-node communication, collective synchronization) that may shift or
fragment regime boundaries.
Our distributed experiments on a 4-node A100 cluster observed a modest
rightward shift ($\Delta\theta_B \approx +0.05$) in the I/O-limited boundary;
fully characterising multi-node regime dynamics remains open.
Second, hybrid training--inference pipelines and highly irregular sparse-access
patterns may exhibit more complex---or nested---regime structures.
Third, the hysteresis-like latency behaviour we observe when workloads traverse
regime boundaries in both directions suggests the presence of \emph{regime
memory} driven by non-linear cache reuse effects; characterising this
phenomenon theoretically and exploiting it in scheduling remains an open
problem.
Finally, while the regime framework identifies boundaries analytically, it does
not yet prescribe optimal orchestration strategies \emph{within} each regime;
combining the boundary theory with workload-specific optimisation is a
promising direction.
