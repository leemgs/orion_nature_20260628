\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern foundation models have shifted the performance frontier of AI inference from arithmetic throughput to \emph{data coordination}. When model weights, activations, and key--value (KV) caches exceed accelerator memory, inference latency is governed less by compute and more by how efficiently execution coordinates data across a hierarchical memory system spanning device memory, host memory, and secondary storage.

This shift carries pressing economic and societal stakes as AI deployment moves from a training-centric to an inference-centric phase. Once a model is trained, the recurring cost of \emph{serving} it---rather than the one-time cost of training---dominates the lifetime resource footprint of deployed AI, and recent hardware analyses argue that the binding constraints of this inference phase are memory capacity, memory bandwidth, and interconnect latency rather than arithmetic throughput~\cite{ma2026inference,gholami2024memorywall,zhou2024survey}. These constraints translate directly into macro-level economic exposure: aggregate capital expenditure across major hyperscalers is on a trajectory to overtake their operating cash inflows around the end of 2026~\cite{juniewicz2026hyperscaler}, so the efficiency with which each inference request is coordinated across the memory hierarchy increasingly governs not only latency but the financial and energy sustainability of large-scale AI services. Industry leadership has likewise characterized memory as the decisive constraint of the AI era, observing that AI infrastructure is absorbing available memory supply faster than it can be produced~\cite{chey2026tpd}. In this context, characterizing the \emph{intrinsic limits} of memory orchestration is not merely a systems-optimization concern but a prerequisite for a viable, inference-driven AI society---precisely the question this paper addresses.

This shift has motivated extensive work on CPU offloading, GPU--CPU swapping, KV-cache management, paging, prefetching, and overlap. However, empirical results are often fragmented: an orchestration strategy that delivers large gains in one configuration can show diminishing returns---or even performance inversion---in another. These failures raise a fundamental question that is not settled by incremental optimization studies: \emph{Is hierarchical memory orchestration a continuously optimizable engineering problem, or does it admit intrinsic regime boundaries beyond which additional coordination cannot improve, and can even worsen, end-to-end latency?}


\paragraph{Prior approaches and why limits remain unclear.}
A large body of systems work reduces memory pressure via CPU/NVMe offloading and heterogeneous execution \cite{flexgen2023,aminabadi2022deepspeedinference}, smart GPU--CPU swapping \cite{huang2020swapadvisor}, and serving-time KV-cache management for long-context batching \cite{kwon2023pagedattention}. While these techniques can deliver substantial gains, reported improvements often vary sharply across model sizes, batch settings, and device/storage configurations. This variability suggests that the governing bottlenecks are not continuously tunable implementation details, but regime-dependent outcomes of capacity balance, sustained cross-tier bandwidth, and unavoidable coordination costs.

We address this question with a regime-based view. We show that multi-tier inference systems exhibit \emph{phase-like} operational regimes governed by dimensionless balances between (i) fast-memory residency and (ii) sustained cross-tier transfer pressure. Crucially, transitions between regimes are \emph{discontinuous}: modest changes in model size, batch size, or available bandwidth can abruptly shift the dominant contributor to latency from locality effects, to coordination overhead, to physical I/O limits. As a result, orchestration mechanisms that appear ``universally beneficial'' within one regime fail predictably outside analytically identifiable boundaries.

\paragraph{Beyond LLM inference.}
We use large language model serving as a canonical stress test, but the regime structure is not LLM-specific. Whenever repeated access requires an active working set that cannot fully reside in fast memory---common in vision and multimodal inference, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), data-intensive analytics, and HPC-style scientific workflows---performance is governed by the same intrinsic balance between residency, sustained transfer bandwidth, and synchronization overhead. Therefore, the regime lens provides a general way to reason about scalability limits in hierarchical-memory computing. Framed against the rising operational cost of inference at scale~\cite{juniewicz2026hyperscaler,chey2026tpd}, regime-aware orchestration is therefore best understood not as incremental tuning but as a lever for the economic and energy viability of memory-bound AI systems.

\paragraph{Scope and role of \textsc{Orion}.}
\textsc{Orion} is introduced as an \emph{experimental probe} to expose and measure regime-dependent behavior, not as a new state-of-the-art inference engine. Our goal is to test the widespread assumption that deeper orchestration is always beneficial: we show that, even with careful coordination, systems cross intrinsic boundaries where additional orchestration cannot further reduce latency. Consequently, improvements observed with \textsc{Orion} should be interpreted as evidence that the operating point lies within a coordination-dominated regime, whereas predictable failure modes beyond regime boundaries illustrate limits that no orchestration policy can eliminate.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Regime-dependent principle.} We identify three intrinsic operational regimes for hierarchical inference---capacity-limited, coordination-dominated, and I/O-limited---and show that transitions are abrupt rather than gradual.
  \item \textbf{Minimal analytical model.} We formalize end-to-end latency as a decomposition into computation, locality-driven memory access, cross-tier transfer, and synchronization overhead, yielding analytically interpretable regime boundaries.
  \item \textbf{Experimental validation.} Using \textsc{Orion} as a measurement instrument, we validate predicted transitions across diverse language and vision workloads and across system configurations, including predictable failure zones beyond coordination-dominated regimes.
\end{itemize}

Together, these results reframe hierarchical memory coordination as a problem of \emph{regime-aware design} rather than continuous tuning, providing a general framework for understanding scalability limits, stability, and energy efficiency in modern AI inference.
